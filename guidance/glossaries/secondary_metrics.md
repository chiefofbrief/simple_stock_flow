# Financial Statements Glossary - Secondary Metrics

---

## OPERATIONS CONTEXT (4)

### 1. Gross Margin (Formula: Gross Profit ÷ Revenue × 100; or (Revenue - COGS) ÷ Revenue × 100)

**Description**
Gross margin reveals pricing power, cost structure efficiency, and competitive positioning before operating expenses. It shows what percentage of each revenue dollar remains after direct costs of production.

**What Margins Reveal**
Margins consistently above peers indicate sustainable competitive advantages such as brand strength, proprietary technology, or cost advantages that competitors cannot easily replicate. High margins suggest strong pricing power or superior cost structure—both indicators of competitive moat strength. Margins below peers but improving suggest turnaround potential as operational improvements take hold. Narrowing margins increase vulnerability—modest price declines or cost increases can quickly produce losses that erode equity.

**Balance Sheet Reconciliation**
The balance sheet must reconcile with reported profitability. If income statement shows strong earnings but net current assets decline faster than dividends paid, earnings are likely overstated through aggressive revenue recognition, understated reserves, or working capital manipulation.

**Red Flags**
- Margins deteriorating (signals worsening prospects and potential management temptation to manipulate earnings)
- Margins narrowing toward breakeven while competitors maintain stable margins
- Strong reported earnings accompanied by working capital deterioration

**Opportunities**
- Margins below peer average but demonstrating consistent improvement quarter-over-quarter
- Temporarily depressed margins in companies with strong competitive positions and historical margin stability
- Margins at or above peer levels with stable trend indicate competitive strength

### 2. Net Margin (Formula: Net Income ÷ Revenue × 100)

**Description**
Net margin shows final profitability after all expenses. However, current earnings can mislead—"indicated earning power" requires examining 5-7 year history (7-10 years for highly cyclical businesses) to understand what the company can reasonably earn under stable conditions.

**Pattern Recognition**
Stable patterns show results clustering near average with moderate variation—the average represents "normal" earning power. Volatile patterns show wide swings producing averages that are merely mathematical artifacts with no tendency for results to approximate them. When the average includes deficit years, question whether it indicates sustainable earning power, as wide variations detract from representativeness.

**Weighting Current Results**
Only weight current figures heavily when persuasive evidence supports continuance: fundamental competitive improvement with demonstrable operational changes, new products with proven market success, or industry conditions that have structurally and permanently improved. Without such evidence, current results may simply reflect temporary boom conditions.

**Quality Indicators**
Wide persistent gaps (>30-40%) between reported earnings and taxable income (calculated as tax accrual ÷ tax rate) demand explanation—legitimate differences exist but large divergences suggest aggressive reporting. Items labeled "nonrecurring" that appear 3+ years over a 5-year period are actually recurring costs that should be included in normalized earning power.

**Red Flags**
- Volatile earnings pattern where average doesn't represent realistic expectation
- Large unexplained GAAP vs. taxable income divergence
- "Nonrecurring" items recurring regularly

**Opportunities**
- Temporarily depressed earnings in businesses with demonstrated long-term earning power, solid competitive positions, and stable historical patterns
- Stable, predictable margins at healthy levels indicate quality earnings

### 3. D&A as % of Revenue (Formula: D&A ÷ Revenue × 100)

**Description**
D&A percentage reveals capital intensity and business model characteristics. High D&A (>10% of revenue) reflects capital-intensive operations requiring ongoing asset replacement, creating a gap between reported earnings and cash flow since depreciation is non-cash.

**The Depreciation Reality**
Depreciation typically understates rather than overstates economic cost in most capital-intensive industries. Assets genuinely wear out and require replacement, often at higher costs due to inflation. Exceptions where assets appreciate (real estate, broadcasting spectrum rights) are rare. Be skeptical of management claims that depreciation is irrelevant—most businesses must eventually replace depreciating assets to maintain competitiveness.

**What It Signals**
Low D&A percentage indicates asset-light operations with minimal capital intensity, typically offering more flexible cost structures and higher returns on capital. High D&A percentage indicates heavy fixed asset base, typically showing more stable but lower returns on capital with higher cyclical volatility.

**What to Look For**
- D&A percentage consistent with business model (manufacturing naturally higher than software)
- Stability in D&A percentage over time (sudden changes may indicate depreciation policy manipulation)
- See Depreciation & Amortization metric for manipulation detection

---

### 4. D&A as % of Operating Cash Flow (Formula: D&A ÷ Operating Cash Flow × 100)

**Description**
This ratio reveals how much of cash generation comes from predictable, mechanical non-cash charges versus actual operational cash collection. Higher ratios (>40%) indicate larger portions of cash generation are programmed and mechanical.

**Why It Matters**
Depreciation provides a cushion for debt service in capital-intensive businesses. Unlike operating earnings, depreciation is a programmed item assured by accounting rules—it's not subject to market vagaries or operational volatility. This creates more stable, dependable cash generation, which is particularly valuable for assessing credit quality. However, the cushion exists only because depreciation involves no current cash outlay—management has wide latitude in setting depreciation rates precisely because there's no check to write.

**What to Look For**
- Higher ratios in capital-intensive businesses providing stability cushion
- Consistency in ratio over time
- See EBIT Coverage for how this cushion translates to debt service capacity

## FINANCIAL HEALTH CONTEXT (8)

### 1. Debt / Total Assets (Formula: Total Debt ÷ Total Assets × 100)

**Description**
This ratio shows what proportion of the asset base is financed with debt versus equity, revealing the company's capital structure and financial risk profile.

**Interpretation Thresholds**
- **30-40%**: Moderate leverage—optimal range for many businesses
- **> 60%**: High leverage—elevated risk but potential for strong equity returns if well-managed
- Rising ratios indicate leveraging up without corresponding earning power growth, potentially impairing credit quality

**What Constitutes Debt**
Permanent short-term debt must be included (many borrowers rely on short-term debt that's never actually repaid but rather continuously renewed). Convertible debt counts as debt until it's actually converted to equity, not when conversion options exist.

**What to Look For**
- Stability in ratio consistent with business model
- Changes in ratio accompanied by proportional changes in earning power
- Comparison to peer group leverage levels
- Moderate leverage in stable businesses can enhance equity returns without excessive risk

---

### 2. Tangible Equity / Total Assets (Formula: (Total Equity - Goodwill - Intangibles) ÷ Total Assets × 100)

**Description**
This ratio reveals how much of the balance sheet consists of tangible, separable assets versus intangibles like goodwill. It matters because goodwill cannot be sold, factored for cash, or used in sale-leaseback arrangements—it's not an asset management can readily convert to cash.

**The Analytical Approach**
Calculate balance sheet ratios both with and without goodwill, giving greater emphasis to the tangibles-only version. Rating agencies follow this practice when assessing credit quality, recognizing recovery values depend on tangible resources. When debt-to-tangible-equity significantly exceeds debt-to-total-equity (e.g., 50% vs. 200%), the large divergence reflects economic reality—goodwill provides no asset protection in financial stress.

**Interpretation Thresholds**
- **> 50%**: Majority of assets tangible and separable—strong asset backing
- **< 30%**: Balance sheet dominated by goodwill—requires scrutiny of intangible value
- Declining ratios driven by acquisitions indicate the balance sheet increasingly reflects acquisition premiums rather than operating assets

**The Modern Context**
Between 1978 and 1999, book value fell from 95% to 71% of market value for U.S. public companies. By 2021, FAANG book values represented just 3-15% of market capitalizations. This widening gap reflects investors paying for intangibles GAAP doesn't capture—brand names, internally developed technology, network effects, customer relationships.

**The Intangibles Paradox**
Modern "intangibles" (brand strength, organizational efficiency, proprietary knowledge) are economically real. Earnings based on intangibles may be even less vulnerable to competition than earnings requiring only capital investment in physical facilities—competitors can't simply replicate them with cash. When conditions are favorable, enterprises with small capital investments often show more rapid growth potential.

However, high intangibles without clear competitive moats warrant skepticism. The key distinction: Does the company have defensible advantages (brand loyalty, network effects, regulatory protection) or merely accounting artifacts from overpaying for acquisitions?

**Red Flags**
- Goodwill > 50% of total assets without clear durable intangible advantages
- Stock-for-stock acquisition alchemy: when stocks rally, acquisitions at high prices create large goodwill, paradoxically "improving" leverage ratios despite acquiring weaker companies
- Declining tangible equity ratio driven by serial acquisitions at high multiples

**Opportunities**
- Tangible equity significantly exceeding market value in viable businesses (market heavily discounts asset backing)
- Strong tangible asset backing provides downside protection and margin of safety

### 3. Current Ratio (Formula: Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities)

**Description**
The current ratio measures short-term liquidity by comparing current assets to current liabilities. The traditional standard of 2:1 (current assets to current liabilities) was formerly minimum for industrial companies; most now exceed this threshold.

**The Acid Test Complement**
Current assets excluding inventory should at least equal current liabilities (1:1). Companies ordinarily should meet both the 2:1 current ratio test and the 1:1 acid test. Ratios approaching the 2:1 threshold reduce buffer before working capital stress; declining toward thresholds while peers maintain comfortable buffers indicates eroding margin of safety.

**Exceptions and Context**
Exceptions to standard thresholds are allowed for seasonal borrowing patterns, temporary needs related to profitable new business initiatives, and industry-specific working capital patterns. Understanding the business model determines whether apparent violations represent genuine weakness or normal operating characteristics.

**The Case for Slack**
The theoretical argument against excess liquidity claims it represents inefficiency—cash trapped in low-return assets. However, three practical counterarguments favor maintaining liquidity cushions:

**Hindsight bias**: Managers may have believed growth forecasts that proved wrong. Had demand grown at expected rates, declining to expand capacity might have cost market share. Failing to keep up with competitors' scale economies could harm shareholders more than temporary excess capacity.

**Operating flexibility**: Cash enables competitive edge when credit is tight. Companies with liquidity cushions can continue essential investments when competitors must retrench, gaining market share and strengthening position during downturns.

**Preserving human capital**: Losing experienced skilled workers through temporary layoffs damages productivity during recovery. Deliberately building inventory to keep workers employed requires working capital adjustment capability that liquidity provides.

**Red Flags**
- Ratio declining toward 2:1 threshold without clear business model justification
- Ratio deteriorating while peers maintain stable levels
- Combination of declining current ratio and rising short-term debt percentage

**Opportunities**
- Strong current ratios (well above 2:1) in fundamentally sound companies trading below intrinsic value indicate financial strength and flexibility
- Comfortable liquidity cushions provide strategic flexibility and downside protection

---

### 4. Quick Ratio (Formula: (Current Assets - Inventory) ÷ Current Liabilities)

**Description**
The quick ratio (acid test) takes a more conservative view of liquidity by excluding inventory, which may not convert to cash readily—particularly if products are obsolete or demand has weakened.

**Interpretation Framework**
The traditional adequate level is 1:1 (current assets excluding inventory equal to current liabilities). Ratios declining below 1:1 indicate current liabilities exceed the most liquid assets, creating potential liquidity stress if inventory cannot be quickly converted. A large gap between current ratio and quick ratio indicates substantial inventory that may not be readily convertible—see Inventory Turnover and Inventory Growth vs. COGS Growth for deeper analysis.

**What to Look For**
- Quick ratio stable or improving over time
- Gap between current and quick ratios consistent with business model (retailers naturally have larger gaps)
- Comparison to peer group quick ratios
- Ratios comfortably above 1:1 indicate strong liquidity position

### 5. EBIT Coverage (Formula: EBIT ÷ Interest Expense)

**Description**
EBIT coverage measures the ability to service fixed interest charges. The distinction between EBIT and EBITDA coverage reveals both short-term debt service capacity and long-term sustainability.

**Time Horizon Distinction**
EBITDA coverage provides insight into short-to-intermediate-term ability to stay current on interest payments. Since depreciation is an accounting entry rather than current cash outlay, a company with negative EBIT can still cover interest from EBITDA—the depreciation cushion buys time. However, EBIT coverage is the truer long-term measure because over longer periods, companies must replace assets giving rise to depreciation.

**The Depreciation Cushion Reality**
Adding back depreciation is appropriate only for the period over which a company can defer substantial capital spending without impairing future competitiveness. Over a full operating cycle, capital expenditures ordinarily at least equal depreciation charges. The depreciation cushion is a temporary safety valve, not a permanent source of coverage.

**Interpretation Thresholds**
- **4-7×**: Adequate for investment-grade—comfortable cushion
- **2-4×**: Moderate buffer—acceptable but less margin for error
- **< 2×**: Minimal buffer—financial stress likely

Coverage declining over time indicates deteriorating debt service capacity. Compare both absolute coverage level and direction of change relative to peer group.

**Red Flags**
- Coverage declining toward 2× threshold
- Company relying on EBITDA coverage while peers comfortably cover interest from EBIT
- Coverage deteriorating due to falling EBIT rather than rising interest expense

**Opportunities**
- Strong coverage (>5-7×) in out-of-favor companies where market focuses on other concerns while overlooking robust debt service capacity
- High coverage provides safety margin and capacity for strategic debt-financed initiatives

### 6. Debt to Tangible Equity (Formula: Total Debt ÷ (Total Equity - Goodwill - Intangibles))

**Description**
This ratio compares debt to equity excluding goodwill and intangibles. When it significantly exceeds total debt-to-equity (e.g., 50% versus 200%), the divergence reflects economic reality—goodwill provides no asset protection in financial stress.

**Why Lenders Calculate Both Ways**
Large divergences indicate most stated equity consists of goodwill. Lenders always calculate both standard leverage and tangible leverage, giving greater weight to tangible measures because recovery values in distress depend on separable assets. High tangible leverage (>100%) indicates limited tangible asset protection for creditors.

**The Synergy Trap**
Goodwill often results from acquisitions justified by "synergy"—possibly the most dangerous word in finance. Expected synergies frequently fail to materialize due to cultural incompatibility, integration challenges, or overestimated cost savings. When companies write down goodwill years after acquisition, it reveals the premium paid was never justified by economic reality.

**Red Flags**
- Debt-to-tangible-equity drastically exceeds debt-to-total-equity
- Negative or negligible tangible equity supporting large market values without clear durable competitive advantages
- Serial acquirer with rising goodwill and deteriorating returns on tangible equity

**Opportunities**
- Companies with solid tangible asset backing selling below tangible book value when market fixates on reported losses or cyclical weakness
- Low tangible leverage provides safety margin and borrowing capacity

### 7. Short-Term Debt as % of Current Liabilities (Formula: Short-Term Debt ÷ Current Liabilities × 100)

**Description**
This ratio reveals dependence on debt requiring imminent repayment or renewal. Financial difficulties are almost always heralded by bank loans or debt due in short time; it's rare for weak financial position to be created solely by trade accounts payable.

**Why It Matters**
Companies in sound financial condition maintain minimal short-term borrowings. Short-term debt persisting year after year carries two distinct risks: refinancing risk (lenders may not renew when market conditions tighten) and interest rate risk (sharp rate increases can skyrocket borrowing costs overnight since rates reset quickly on short-term debt).

**The Maturity Restructuring Warning**
When a company scrambles to convert short-term debt to long-term, it signals management has lost control. This restructuring reveals management is making at best a highly risky bet or at worst a calculated effort to disguise deteriorating operations by buying time before facing constraints.

**Red Flags**
- Proportion >30% or rising over time
- Sudden shift from short-term to long-term debt during period of operational difficulties
- Short-term debt percentage rising while OCF declines

**Opportunities**
- Low short-term debt percentage indicates financial stability and reduced refinancing risk

### 8. Cash & Equivalents as % of Total Assets (Formula: Cash & Equivalents ÷ Total Assets × 100)

**Description**
This ratio measures liquidity cushion and strategic flexibility. However, interpretation is highly context-dependent—excess cash held by operating companies often trades at 50-70% of face value as markets fear management will deploy it poorly.

**Strategic Value of Cash**
Cash provides multiple competitive advantages during difficult periods: continue essential investments when competitors must retrench, avoid painful external financing when interest rates are high or stock prices depressed, gain edge when tight credit makes working capital financing difficult for others, make opportunistic acquisitions when credit-dependent competitors cannot act. During credit crunches, external financing becomes unavailable at any price; self-funding companies never face this existential constraint.

**Management Behavior**
Management is naturally loath to return capital even when it would be more valuable in shareholders' hands. Returning capital means curtailing resources, potentially creating financial problems later, and reducing officers' prestige and scope of control. This behavioral tendency causes markets to discount excess cash held within corporations.

**Interpretation Thresholds**
- **5-15% of annual revenue**: Typical operating needs
- **> 15% of assets**: May indicate excess beyond operating requirements
- **> 10-20% of market cap**: Substantial "trapped" value trading at discount (requires verifying operating business remains viable)

**Red Flags**
- Very high cash percentage combined with poor returns on assets (suggests management hoarding rather than efficiently deploying capital)
- Cash as percentage of assets rising while returns on total assets decline
- Cash accumulation without clear strategic deployment plan

**Opportunities**
- Large cash positions in out-of-favor companies where market heavily discounts "trapped" cash value, creating hidden asset value
- Cash-rich companies in industries facing credit constraints when competitors' capital access is impaired
- Strong cash positions provide strategic flexibility and downside protection

## EFFICIENCY CONTEXT (5)

### 1. Receivables Growth vs. Revenue Growth (Formula: Receivables Growth % - Revenue Growth %)

**Description**
This comparison reveals whether receivables are accumulating faster than sales, signaling either customer payment problems or loosening credit standards—both negative for asset quality and earnings quality.

**What Divergence Signals**
When receivables grow substantially faster than revenue, three explanations exist: (1) customers paying more slowly because they're encountering financial difficulties, (2) company extending generous payment terms to boost sales (buying revenue through credit liberalization), or (3) revenue being recognized before cash collectability is assured (aggressive accounting). All three indicate deteriorating business fundamentals or accounting quality.

**The Threshold Warning**
Receivables growing >1.5× revenue growth signals potential problems requiring investigation. This threshold provides early warning before problems become severe. A/R growing substantially faster than revenue without clear business model change (new distribution channel, geographic expansion with different payment terms, shift to new customer segment) constitutes red flag.

**Combined Analysis Warning**
When both receivables AND inventory balloon simultaneously, it creates double warning signal—the company is both offering credit AND unable to ship products, suggesting demand weakness masked by aggressive sales practices. See DSO for absolute collection efficiency measure to validate whether divergence represents acceleration or consistently slow collection.

**Red Flags**
- Receivables growing >1.5× revenue growth
- Divergence accelerating over multiple periods
- Combined with inventory growth acceleration
- Divergence without clear business model explanation

**Opportunities**
- Companies with receivables growth consistently lagging revenue growth demonstrate efficient working capital management and high-quality revenue

---

### 2. DSO (Days Sales Outstanding) (Formula: (Accounts Receivable ÷ Revenue) × 365)

**Description**
DSO measures collection efficiency by calculating how many days of sales are tied up in receivables. It provides absolute efficiency level to validate patterns seen in Receivables Growth vs. Revenue Growth analysis.

**Interpretation and Thresholds**
DSO >60 days or >20% worse than peers indicates collection issues. DSO increasing while peers remain stable signals deteriorating collection efficiency or loosening credit standards—either way, it indicates asset quality deterioration. The metric shows in absolute terms whether receivables represent normal business practices or problematic accumulation.

**Business Model Context**
Software companies and subscription businesses typically sell through licenses with cash paid in advance, making high DSO particularly suspicious in these business models. The working capital model fundamentally differs from companies extending credit as standard practice. Compare DSO to business model peers, not to different business models—enterprise software company's DSO shouldn't be compared to industrial distributor's DSO.

**Red Flags**
- Bloated receivables balances are classic red flag—sales not turning into cash receipts, possibly because fictitious
- Days receivables extending significantly beyond industry norms without explanation
- DSO rising while management claims improved sales quality or customer base

**Opportunities**
- Low DSO relative to peers indicates efficient collection and high-quality revenue
- Stable DSO over time demonstrates consistent working capital management

### 3. Inventory Growth vs. COGS Growth (Formula: Inventory Growth % - COGS Growth %)

**Description**
This comparison reveals whether inventory is accumulating faster than sales, suggesting demand weakness, production mismanagement, or inadequate obsolescence reserves.

**What Divergence Signals**
When inventory grows faster than COGS, several negative implications exist: potential accumulation of unsalable goods, production not adjusted downward to match declining sales, or inadequate reserves for obsolescence meaning current earnings are overstated. Working capital needed represents fairly constant percentage of sales—deviations signal problems. Material increase in inventories as percentage of sales is red flag indicating company may be postponing write-offs of unsalable merchandise.

**The Inventory Paradox**
From company viewpoint, taking inventory losses equals operating loss—a negative event. From investor viewpoint (if the investor valued inventory in their own mind well below book value), what appears as loss may actually constitute profit—the company converted assets to cash at better realization than the conservative investor expected.

The distinction matters: decline can result from physical quantity reduction (actual liquidation) versus unit price decline (marking down the same physical quantity). Half of inventory declines during depressions often result solely from unit prices, essentially revaluation rather than true liquidation.

**The Threshold Warning**
Inventory growing >1.5× COGS growth signals accumulation problems indicating demand issues or inventory management failures. This threshold triggers deeper investigation into business fundamentals and management actions.

**Combined Analysis Warning**
Combined with receivables growth acceleration, inventory accumulation creates particularly dangerous signal. The company faces dual working capital problems: can't collect cash from customers (receivables building) and can't ship products (inventory building). See Inventory Turnover for absolute efficiency validation.

**Red Flags**
- Inventory growing >1.5× COGS growth
- Inventory as percentage of sales rising materially
- Inventory turnover deteriorating versus industry peers
- Combined with receivables growth acceleration

**Opportunities**
- Inventory declining faster than sales may indicate channel clearing or improving efficiency, potentially signaling operational improvement

---

### 4. Inventory Turnover (Formula: COGS ÷ Average Inventory)

**Description**
Inventory turnover measures how quickly inventory converts to sales. Using COGS rather than revenue provides better measure because both numerator and denominator are based on cost, while revenue includes markup that can distort the physical turnover rate.

**Interpretation and Thresholds**
Turnover <4× or significantly below peer averages indicates slowing movement. Slowing turnover reflects either accumulation of slow-moving products (obsolescence, fashion misses, demand shifts) or production exceeding sales (manufacturing not adjusted to demand reality). The metric provides absolute efficiency level to validate patterns seen in Inventory Growth vs. COGS Growth analysis.

**What to Look For**
- Turnover stability relative to historical patterns
- Comparison to peer group turnover rates
- Changes in turnover accompanied by management explanations of business model shifts
- Consistency between turnover trends and inventory growth patterns

**Opportunities**
- High turnover relative to peers indicates operational efficiency
- Improving turnover demonstrates strengthening inventory management

### 5. Capex / Depreciation (Formula: Capital Expenditures ÷ Depreciation & Amortization)

**Description**
This ratio shows whether capital spending merely replaces depreciating assets or exceeds replacement for growth investment. It reveals management's investment intensity and whether the company is maintaining, harvesting, or expanding its asset base.

**The Adequacy Question**
Ratio <1.0 over several years raises red flag suggesting failure to replace plant and equipment—underspending on capital replacement amounts to gradual liquidation of the firm. However, can't assume all is well simply because capex consistently exceeds depreciation. Three complications arise: (1) persistent inflation means nominal dollar today won't buy as much capacity as when the depreciating asset was acquired, (2) depreciation may be understated regarding wear-and-tear or obsolescence, (3) capex may be too low even if matching depreciation—growth industry company failing to expand at competitor rates may lose economies of scale and fall victim to shakeout.

**Long-Term Reality**
Over a full operating cycle, capital expenditures ordinarily at least equal depreciation charges. In practice, capex usually exceeds depreciation due to expansion for growth and inflation in equipment costs. The D in EBITDA provides temporary safety valve but not permanent coverage—companies must eventually replace assets to maintain competitiveness.

**Discretionary Cuts and Trade-offs**
Companies with discretionary cash flow below dividends have additional cushion in capital budget cuts. Retailers can reduce store additions and defer refurbishment; industrials can drop to maintenance-level capex (keeps equipment working but no capacity additions or modernization). However, competitiveness trade-offs emerge: retailers find sales drop if stores look tired; industrial companies lose competitive edge if they drop to maintenance-level spending for extended periods.

Analyst should seek independent confirmation of maintenance level figure from engineer familiar with the business—companies may exaggerate extent they can reduce capital spending to conserve cash during downturns.

**Long-Term Damage from Cuts**
Many items a company can cut without disrupting short-run operations are essential to long-term health. Advertising and research are obvious targets for cutbacks since benefits are visible only in future periods while costs are apparent in current period. Over many years, a company that habitually scrimps on such expenditures can impair competitiveness, transforming short-term problem into long-term one. The current period may show improved margins while the future is being mortgaged.

**Red Flags**
- Capex/Depreciation <1.0 persistently (gradual liquidation)
- PP&E growing substantially faster than revenue, especially when revenue growth decelerating (signals either fraud through capitalizing expenses, management overconfidence, or poorly conceived expansion)
- Capital cuts when competitors maintain or increase spending (sacrificing competitive position)
- Capex cuts combined with margin improvement (potentially mortgaging future for current results)

**Opportunities**
- Ratio >1.5 indicates growth investment beyond replacement needs, potentially building competitive advantages
- Companies maintaining strong capex during downturns may be building competitive advantage while competitors retrench
- Consistent capex exceeding depreciation in growing markets indicates reinvestment for expansion
